GameIn

Connecting brands and gamers worldwide.

Role:

UX Researcher

Tools:

Figma

Context

GameIn aims to provide an established integrated team find, sponsorship, certification, and transfer market platform in the gaming and eSports industry. The vision for the platform strives to define GameIn as a leading matchmaking system between eSports teams and sponsorship organizations. It serves as a "one-stop-shop" for endemic and non-endemic stakeholders looking for teams, influencers, athletes, coaches, partners, and investment opportunities in order to improve their business and achieve strategic goals.

As a development team under GameIn, our purpose is to enhance and further the platform's existing user experience and user interface through various combined skill sets, ultimately shaping the platform into an even more engaging and interactive system.

Problem Statement

Due to limited access to counselors, numerous students lack the necessary support to express their nervousness about transitioning to college. However, relying on chatbots to address these needs can create conflicts as chatbots primarily focus on providing resources or quick responses to inquiries, often overlooking the emotional needs of students.

How might we design an authentic chatbot that can provide highschool students a support system to express their concerns regarding the challenging transition to college?

The Design Process

Requirements Document

When creating GameIn, we wanted to make sure we clearly understood what the sponsors were asking us to do, so to clearly visualize this, we created a written document containing all the requirements of the project details.

In this document, we planned a layout of what pages we would need and the possible content that would be on each page. After double checking with our sponsorâ€™s that our requirements document aligned with their vision, we proceeded to create a user flow diagram to better visualize the information in the document.

User Flow

The user flow diagrams helped to envision the way users would navigate through GameIn, and plan for potential differences depending on the userâ€™s main goal (organization, team, or player).

We mainly focused on creating 4 pages: Create Teams Page, User Profile Page, a Dashboard Page, and a Chat Page.

User flow diagram

Wireframes

After finalizing the flow and content of each page, we began working on converting user flows into wireframes. In one of our earlier iterations of the create team page, which prompts the user to link their social media account, we ran into problems concerning the adding of accounts and design of the progress bar; there was no way to add multiple accounts of the same social media, and the minimalism of the progress bar was not very informative. We fixed these issues by adding a popout which allowed users to add as many accounts as they liked, and upgraded the progress bar to more easily convey to the user where exactly in the create team process they are by adding numbered steps and subject labeling. Our low-fidelity mockups allowed us to visualize this and make changes in the early stages so we can avoid further problems in later design stages.

Lo-Fi Wireframes

After more rounds of feedback we created mid fidelity wireframes.

Mid-Fi Wireframes

Prototyping

After our mid-fidelity wireframes were approved, they were sent over to the graphic designer on the team who mocked up high fidelity renditions of the design. Once he completed these, they were sent back to the team so they could be coded into a functioning prototype.

Using ReactJS as our coding language and Amazon AWS to host our project, we were able to collaboratively work on the coding process. We closely followed the mockups made by the Graphic Designer and made sure to also keep in mind usability guidelines.

Reflections

Challenges

When I first joined this project, I was less familiar with front-end development, and found it a bit challenging to switch from the design process into the middle coding process. However, as I continued to research React and started communicating with the coding lead for clarification, I was able to work quicker and got more used to the coding process as well. Overall, I think this was an amazing experience that got me out of my comfort zone but in a good way!

Another challenge was that oftentimes, the team would work remotely at separate times. This would sometimes make it difficult to note what was being worked on, what had been completed by someone else, and what still needed to be completed. In order to fix this, however, we were able to update a sprint sheet with our assigned tasks and also delegate tasks in our in person meetings.

Reflection

Working on GameIn was a very big opportunity for me to work on my design, coding, and communication skills all at once. I had the opportunity to work closely with the sponsor; this allowed me to voice my thoughts on existing designs, design new mockups, and allowed me to remain open for feedback in case any changes had to be made.

One main thing that I learned from this experience was to be open to feedback at any stage of design. When designing, we had a lot of freedom of how we wanted to represent certain pages, so the feedback sessions from the sponsor were very important to letting us know what elements we should keep or change in order to satisfy what they actually wanted or envisioned.

Another thing I learned was to always communicate with the team. Oftentimes, our team would work at different times, so in order for everyone to be on the same page after working hours, we began to keep a log of assigned tasks in a sprintsheet and delegated these tasks in our group meeting times. This made sure to keep everything organized and evenly distributed tasks throughout the team.
