1. Intro

Who are we?

We are the Practical Measures, Routines and Representations (PMR2) design team.

The PMR2 is a collaborative project between university researchers and school district partners to develop a system of practical measures, routines and representations to support the implementation of instructional improvement strategies in middle-grades mathematics. As the design team for Edsight, our job is to ensure each interface is accessible and consistent in design.

What is Edsight?

A new way to visualize data

Edsight is a free data analytics platform designed to assist educators in pedagogical improvements (specifically mathematical courses) through data and reflection. Edsight is designed to fit current practices of educators. It facilitates conversations around pedagogical improvements for teachers, facilitators, and district leaders.

Problem Statement

Why Edsight needed a design system

The absence of a design system for Edsight proved to be a significant problem when we attempt to iterate designs. The issue of consistency and lack of accessibility in the designs decreased the coherence of the site and our productivity. To resolve these issues, we spent some time developing an accessible and well-thought out design system for Edsight.

2. The Process

Research

Challenges to making a design system

A key point of emphasis we kept in mind while building our design system was accessibility. We wanted to ensure that users with different backgrounds will be able to easily interact with our software.

We focused on testing the color contrast for all of our brand colors to make sure we had sufficient contrast across all of our components. This allows users to easily differentiate between different components. Additionally, we focused on choosing a font that has easily distinguishable height differences and characters so that it is readable in different contexts.

Research

Brainstorming the moodboard

To establish the major themes and style of our design system, we created a moodboard to collect inspiration and help brainstorm different ideas. From our brainstorming sessions, we identified a few key pillars for our design system: friendly, schoolhouse, handwritten, warm, and in progress.

With Edsight being a tool for teachers to use in the classroom, we wanted to make sure that our design system reflected the aesthetics and atmosphere of a classroom. We took inspiration from different items that are typically found in a classroom. However, we also looked at other education softwares for modern inspiration. By doing so, we were able to create a comprehensive moodboard to guide us throughout the creation of our design system.

3. Design Decisions

Color Palette

When selecting our new color palette, we had two main items in mind: accessibility & brand colors.

We wanted to make sure the colors were distinct enough from each other to uphold the standard for color blindness tests, as well as have a high enough color contrast ratio with our background for comfortable usability. We tested various color combinations prior to settling on our 9 main colors.

Typography

When selecting typography, we championed clarity.

When selecting typography, we championed clarity. We compared o to 0 and I to l to 1 for legibility and distinguishability, before settling on the work sans font. In the comparisons of often mistaken characters, we looked for how distinguishable these characters were from each other to assess clarity. The Work Sans font ultimately had the clearest readability since it ensured each character was distinct and legible.

Icons

We wanted simple designs with recognizable imagery to help users intuitively understand what each icon represents.

The icons chosen for our branding are very simple and minimalistic in their design. We wanted simple designs with recognizable imagery to help users intuitively understand what each icon represents.

Animations

We wanted simple designs with recognizable imagery to help users intuitively understand what each icon represents.

For our animation designs, we used simple designs that could easily be identified as symbols for loading. Progress bars and loading dots are often associated with loading screens so users will be able to recognize these two concepts and more easily identify them as loading animations. In addition, they are colored with our branding’s color palette and fit to the minimalist theme.

4. Before & After

Navigation

Our navigation bar underwent many iterations before we settled on this current design.

The main purpose of the navigation bar is to allow the user to move easily between different classes and navigate to their desired location. The expandable doubled navigation bar provides flexibility without losing the ease of access.

Each navigation tab has an icon and text associated with it to avoid miscommunication and allow users to directly know what each tab represents. We found that icons alone can leave users wondering what the tab represents and ultimately make the navigation process harder and more time-consuming. Therefore, by adding simple text describing the page, the understandability of the tab navigation increases drastically.

5. Lessons Learned

Reflection

1. Keep Human Centered Design practices into account when implementing our ideas into the mockups.

Reflection

2. Accessibility needs to be carefully considered in all design choices.

After evaluating and critiquing our sketches within our group, we found that there were changes to be made in our sketches to include into our mid-fi wireframes, as well as other features we wanted to include. Our full feature list includes: onboarding, plant encyclopedia, reminders, plant scanner, explore, and user profile.

Reflection

3. Even with a design system, communication between designers is important to maintain consistency.

Care Schedules

Many users wanted help with keeping a consistent schedule for their plant care. To address this, we implemented a reminder & notification feature that will notify users of any immediate tasks, upcoming tasks, and prior tasks for each of their plants.
